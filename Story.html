Story

What has become a sparse speck of land in rural Alaska used to be a wide coastal

Native American tribe of Kivalina, which now looks as though it is a shadow of

its former self. After generations of nomadic life, the townsfolk have been forced

to settle, despite the tremendous hurdles they face due to climate change and

water pollution. This small tribehas only ten houses with running water, yet even

then the owners of those houses must empty pots of sewage in a landfill to avoid

contamination.

The tribe of Kivalina consists of a small strip of land on the coast.

This sandy strip becomes ever-smaller, however, due to the rising water levels

caused by climate change. The tribe attempts to shore up this issue by creating

a sandbag barrier which has done little, if anything, to protect them. At this

point, the villagers would leave the area in a heartbeat, however, due to schools

set up by the Bureau of Indian affairs, the Natives are bound there, as anyone who

left could face imprisonment and loss of their children, making escape effectively

impossible.

The tribe of Kivalina attempted to strike back at the issues they

faced by suing Exxonmobil, Chevron, BP, and a multitude of other oil and energy

organizations on the grounds that the effects of the environment that were harming

the village were the fault of these companies. The court ruled against Kivalina,

stating that an issue of this scale would need to be fixed with legislation, as a

correlation between the companies activities and Kivalina’s struggles could not be

confirmed. The tribe decided to appeal, yet the verdict did not change.

The rising water levels were just one of the tribe’s major struggles. Water found

in the area of Kivalina had been contaminated with a sort of orange goo. There was

initial speculation as to whether the goo could be rust particles or fungal spores.

This made many areas in the tribe unsafe to drink from or swim in, for fear of

contamination.

To this day, the members of Kivalina have not been adequately

helped, and must rely on hope. Despite doing no harm, the United States has

largely turned a blind eye to theirstruggles and the struggles of similar tribes.

One village member stated that We're United States citizens. We're the taxpayers.

We have military that serve in the Iraq war. So we're part of United States. If

we're being wiped out, you know, who's going to replace us?
